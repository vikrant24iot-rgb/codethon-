Cyber Safety FAQs

Find answers tailored to your specific needs.

For Students

Q: How can I stay safe when using public Wi-Fi on campus or at a coffee shop?

A: Public Wi-Fi is often unsecured, so never use it for sensitive activities like online banking or shopping. When connecting, make sure your device's firewall is on and consider using a VPN (Virtual Private Network) to encrypt your data.

Q: Is it safe to use my school's computers for personal logins?

A: Be cautious. If you log into a personal account on a shared computer, be sure to log out completely and clear the browser's history and cache. It's best to use your own device for personal data whenever possible.

Q: What's the best way to handle social media privacy when living on campus?

Q: What is social engineering, and how can I avoid it in a school setting?

A: Social engineering is a way for cybercriminals to trick people into giving them personal information. This could be a fake email from the "IT department" asking for your password or a text message offering a free gift card if you click a link. Always be suspicious of unexpected requests for information, and never share your passwords.

Q: How important is a strong password, and how can I create one that's easy to remember but hard to guess?

A: A strong password is your first line of defense. Instead of using a single word, try creating a "passphrase" by combining four or more random words, like PurpleTableCorrectHorse. This makes it long and complex, but still memorable.

Q: Are online learning platforms secure? What should I watch out for?

A: Reputable platforms are generally secure, but you should still be careful. Avoid clicking links in chat messages from unknown users. Make sure your browser is updated, and if your school provides a two-factor authentication option, use it.

Q: What should I do if my laptop or phone is stolen?

A: Act quickly. First, report the theft to campus security and the police. If you have "Find My Device" enabled, try to locate it or remotely wipe the data. Change all your passwords, especially for school and banking accounts, as soon as possible.

For Professionals

Q: My company allows remote work. How do I protect corporate data from home?

A: Always use your company-issued device for work. Ensure your home Wi-Fi network is secure and password-protected. Use a VPN to access company networks and never download work files to personal cloud storage or devices.

Q: How can I spot a "spear phishing" email that targets me specifically?

A: Spear phishing emails are highly personalized. Be suspicious of emails that create a sense of urgency, request unusual wire transfers, or ask for your login credentials. Always verify requests through a separate channel, like a phone call, before acting.

Q: Is it safe to use public charging stations at airports and hotels?

A: No, these can be a security risk. Public charging stations are susceptible to "juice jacking," where malware can be installed on your device. Always use a wall outlet with your own charger or carry a portable power bank.

Q: How do I handle suspicious attachments or links in work emails?

A: Never click on an attachment or link from an unknown sender. If the sender is known but the email seems unusual, contact them directly through a different channel (like a phone call or a separate instant message) to verify the request before clicking anything.

Q: What is a "man-in-the-middle" attack, and how does it affect me when traveling for work?

A: A man-in-the-middle attack happens when a hacker intercepts communication between your device and a server. This is most common on public Wi-Fi. To protect yourself, always use your company's VPN when connected to public networks to encrypt your data.

Q: My company requires me to use two-factor authentication (2FA). Why is this so important?

A: 2FA adds a critical layer of security. Even if a cybercriminal steals your password, they can't access your account without the second factor, such as a code sent to your phone or generated by an app.

Q: How can I safely manage my passwords for multiple work accounts?

A: Use a secure, company-approved password manager. These tools generate and store complex, unique passwords for all your accounts, so you only have to remember one master password.

For Homemakers

Q: I do a lot of online shopping. How can I make sure my payments are secure?

A: Only shop on reputable websites with "https://" in the URL and a padlock icon in the address bar. Avoid using public Wi-Fi for purchases and use strong, unique passwords for each shopping account. Consider using a credit card with fraud protection for all online transactions.

Q: What should I do if I get an email about a package that I didn't order?

A: This is a common scam. Do not click on any links in the email. Instead, go directly to the official website of the delivery company (like FedEx or UPS) and track your package using the tracking number you know is valid, or contact them directly.

Q: We have smart home devices (thermostats, cameras). How do I keep them safe from hackers?

A: Change the default passwords on all smart devices immediately after setting them up. Keep the firmware on all devices updated. If a device has a camera or microphone, consider disabling it or placing it in a separate room when not in use.

Q: My family's gaming consoles and streaming services seem to be a target for hackers. How can I protect them?

A: Change all default passwords immediately. Use a strong, unique password for each device and account. Enable parental controls, and if possible, use two-factor authentication on all gaming and streaming services.

Q: My kids are always online. How do I teach them about cyber safety without scaring them?

A: Start with simple, positive rules, like "don't talk to strangers online" and "ask a parent before you download a new game." Teach them what a scam looks like by showing them examples of phishing emails, and encourage them to always tell you if something online makes them feel uncomfortable.

Q: My social media has been hacked. What should I do first?

A: First, try to regain control of your account by using the "forgot password" or "account recovery" feature. Inform your friends and family on a different platform (like text or phone call) that your account has been compromised and they should ignore any messages from it. Report the hack to the social media platform's support team.

Q: What is "ransomware" and how can I protect my family's photos and files from it?

A: Ransomware is malicious software that locks up your files and demands a payment to unlock them. To protect against it, back up all your important files regularly to an external hard drive or a secure cloud service. This way, if you are attacked, you won't have to pay the ransom to get your data back.

For Senior Citizens

Q: My grandchild's Facebook account is asking me for money. How can I know it's really them?

A: This is a very common "grandparent scam." A hacker may have taken over your grandchild's account. Do not send any money. Call your grandchild directly on a number you know is theirs to verify the request. Never rely solely on social media messages for such requests.

Q: I received a call from someone saying they are from Microsoft and need to fix a problem on my computer. Is this real?

A: This is a classic "tech support scam." Legitimate companies like Microsoft will never call you out of the blue to fix a problem. Hang up immediately. Do not give them remote access to your computer or your personal information.

Q: How can I tell if a website is trustworthy before entering my credit card information?

A: Look for the padlock icon and "https://" in the URL. Check the company's contact information and look for reviews online. Be wary of websites with poor grammar or an unprofessional design.

Q: What is the safest way to store my passwords so I don't forget them?

A: The safest way is to use a password manager. If you're not comfortable with that, write them down in a notebook and keep it in a secure, private place, like a locked drawer. Never save passwords in a document on your computer or phone, or on a sticky note.

Q: I get a lot of pop-up ads saying my computer has a virus. What should I do?

A: These pop-ups are almost always a scam. Do not click on them or call the number provided. Close the browser window by pressing `Ctrl + W` (on Windows) or `Cmd + W` (on Mac). If the pop-up won't close, you may need to shut down your computer and restart it.

Q: What is a "phishing" email, and how can I recognize one?

A: Phishing is when a scammer sends a fake email to trick you into giving up personal information. Look for common signs like poor grammar, urgent threats, requests for personal information, or links that don't match the company's official website.
